Solar energetic particle (SEP) events are a principal radiation hazard for lunar surface operations: regolith overburden protects a buried habitat but not surface infrastructure, rovers, or crew on EVA. Active magnetic shielding proposals have concentrated on a single architecture — the compact monolithic dipole (artificial mini-magnetosphere) — which protects by pressure standoff. This work assesses an alternative: a distributed array of modest superconducting coils forming a perimeter fence, which attenuates by repeated scattering, as the heliosphere does.

Verified relativistic Boris-pusher test-particle simulations of protons (0.1–100 MeV) show that, at equal total magnetic moment (4×10¹⁰ A·m²), a 22-coil two-channel fence matches the monolithic dipole: folded through a representative soft SEP spectrum, both remove ≈95% of proton number fluence and 72–75% of energy fluence, while the array leaves the habitat field-free and degrades gracefully under single-coil failure. Parameter sweeps yield design scaling laws: the rigidity knee follows E₅₀ ∝ M² in moment budget, and attenuation is governed by the largest aperture in the fence. A helically wound variant exhibits handedness-dependent transmission — up to a factor of ten between winding senses at 3 MeV — which switches on only above a geometric sampling threshold (pitch greater than twice the site spacing) and is offered as a new, controllable degree of freedom in active shield design. First-order engineering estimates (≈12 t of REBCO conductor, ≈260 MJ stored energy, coil fields of a few mT) place the concept within near-term high-temperature superconductor capability.

This deposit contains the full manuscript, the complete simulation suite (Python 3/NumPy: relativistic Boris integrator, geometry builders, validation and convergence tests, parameter sweeps, and SEP spectrum folding), the transmission datasets (CSV/JSON), and all figures. Simulations are collisionless, single-particle, and magnetostatic; plasma-kinetic (PIC) and dose-transport (HZETRN/Geant4) analyses are identified as required follow-on work.
